# Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC) Method

## 1. Overview

The Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC) method is a melt-growth crystal-pulling technique used to produce single crystals of compound semiconductors whose constituents have a high vapor pressure at their melting point — most notably the III–V compounds GaAs, InP, and GaP, as well as II-VI/IV-VI materials such as PbSe and PbTe. It is a direct modification of the conventional Czochralski (Cz) pulling technique, adapted specifically to suppress the dissociation and evaporative loss of a volatile component (As, P, Se, Te) from the melt during growth.

## 2. Historical Background

Conventional Czochralski pulling, invented by Jan Czochralski in 1916 and adapted for semiconductors by Teal and Little in the early 1950s, works well for elemental semiconductors like Si and Ge, which have negligible vapor pressure at their melting points. Compound semiconductors posed a serious problem: an early workaround by Gremmelmaier in the early 1950s enclosed the entire pulling apparatus in a sealed silica chamber pressurized with 1–2 bar of arsenic vapor, using a magnetically coupled, contactless pulling rod to avoid breaking the seal. The commercial breakthrough came with the Liquid Encapsulation Czochralski technique invented by Metz et al., where the melt and crystal are simply covered by a layer of molten glassy boric oxide, using an arsenic overpressure for GaAs or phosphorus for InP. This innovation (often attributed to Mullin and coworkers in the mid-1960s) eliminated the need for a fully sealed, pressurized silica ampoule around the whole puller and made industrial-scale production of GaAs and InP boules practical.

## 3. Working Principle

If the material to be grown has a high partial pressure of one or more components at the melting point, a modified Czochralski setup is used, called the Liquid Encapsulated Czochralski method. In this setup a liquid, usually boric oxide (B₂O₃), is applied to encapsulate the melt and the growing crystal, preventing evaporation of the volatile component. The pressure of the inert gas in the growth chamber must exceed the partial pressures of the volatile components to keep the encapsulant layer stable and prevent boiling/decomposition of the melt underneath it.

The molten B₂O₃ layer floats on top of the denser semiconductor melt (a consequence of B₂O₃'s lower density) and forms a continuous liquid seal. As the seed crystal is dipped through this layer and withdrawn upward, the growing crystal is pulled up through the encapsulant, which coats the crystal surface as a thin glassy film and protects it from the ambient atmosphere until it has cooled enough that dissociation is no longer a concern.

## 4. Apparatus and Process Components

**Crucible and charge:** A pBN (pyrolytic boron nitride) or quartz crucible holds the polycrystalline starting charge (e.g., GaAs, InP), typically synthesized beforehand by methods such as the horizontal Bridgman or boat-growth technique.

**Encapsulant:** A layer of B₂O₃, typically several centimeters thick chosen in the range of 2–5 cm before the start of growth, is placed above the charge. B₂O₃ is chosen for its chemical inertness, low melting point (~450°C), transparency (allowing visual monitoring of the growth interface), and ability to remain liquid and impermeable across the relevant temperature range.

**Pressure vessel:** The entire crucible assembly sits inside a sealed, water-cooled steel autoclave capable of withstanding high inert-gas pressures. High-pressure LEC pullers can operate up to 100 bar, allowing growth of crystals up to about 4 inches in diameter and roughly 30 cm in length.

**Heating system:** A resistive heating system, often using a multi-heater arrangement of several graphite heaters, reaches temperatures up to about 1800°C, sufficient to melt the charge (GaAs melts at ~1238°C, InP at ~1062°C) and maintain the B₂O₃ layer molten.

**Inert atmosphere:** High-purity nitrogen or argon fills the chamber under pressure. The melt is pressurized by inert gas such as nitrogen, with its surface covered by molten boron oxide to prevent decomposition and vaporization of the compound semiconductor melt.

**Pulling mechanism:** A seed crystal, mounted on a rotating pull rod, is lowered through the B₂O₃ layer to contact the melt surface, then slowly withdrawn while both the crystal and crucible counter-rotate, controlling diameter, thermal symmetry, and dopant distribution.

## 5. Process Variants

- **Low-pressure LEC (LP-LEC):** Uses moderate inert-gas overpressure; because it uses GaAs polycrystal formed by the boat-growth method as starting material, the single crystal tends to suffer inclusion of impurities (predominantly silicon) and typically requires chromium doping to impart semi-insulating properties, though Cr doping above certain concentrations introduces segregation and thermal-conversion defects.
- **High-pressure LEC (HP-LEC):** Uses gas pressures exceeding the full dissociation pressure of the compound (tens of bar), allowing direct synthesis of the compound from elemental Ga/In and As/P inside the same puller, followed by in-situ crystal growth without a separate synthesis step.
- **Related encapsulated variants:** Liquid-encapsulated vertical Bridgman (LE-VB) and liquid-encapsulated vertical gradient freeze (LE-VGF) methods apply the same B₂O₃ encapsulation principle but with different bulk solidification geometries, reducing the temperature gradient at the growth boundary and yielding lower dislocation densities than standard LEC pulling.

## 6. Materials Grown

Today, the LEC technique is the most widely used growth method for the compound semiconductors GaAs, GaP, and InP, as well as for PbSe and PbTe. It is used for producing single crystals of gallium phosphide (GaP), gallium arsenide (GaAs), and indium phosphide (InP) — materials foundational to optoelectronics (LEDs, laser diodes), high-frequency RF devices, and photovoltaic substrates.

## 7. Crystal Quality Challenges

**Thermal gradients and dislocations:** The low thermal conductivity of the liquid encapsulant causes large temperature gradients and temperature nonlinearities in the growing crystal, which is unfavorable for crystal quality since it generates a relatively large number of structural defects (dislocations).

**Twinning:** Nucleation in twin orientation on edge facets is a known failure mode during LEC growth of III–V compounds. Conditions can be derived under which nucleation in twin orientation is thermodynamically favored over correct-orientation nucleation during Czochralski growth of semiconductors, based on the requirement that edge facets be anchored at the three-phase boundary; these conditions are satisfied for GaAs, InP, and InSb but not for Ge or Si. Practical guidelines to avoid twinning in InP growth include using starting material free of fine InP particles, controlling the crystal's cone angle to less than 19.68° relative to the growth axis, and arranging the hot zone so the encapsulant's top-surface temperature stays below 550°C.

**Dopant and impurity control:** Electrical properties of nominally undoped LEC crystals are often dominated by residual silicon impurity, whose concentration in the crystal corresponds to that of the starting material, and dislocation densities in undoped LEC InP depend strongly on thermal stresses during growth — insight that has enabled growth of essentially dislocation-free crystals under optimized conditions.

**Boron incorporation:** Because the crystal is pulled directly through the B₂O�3 layer, some boron can be incorporated into the growing crystal, an effect that has also been deliberately exploited as an intentional doping route in some process patents.

## 8. Comparison with Alternative Techniques

Vertical gradient freeze (VGF) and vertical Bridgman methods have emerged as lower-thermal-gradient alternatives to LEC pulling for GaAs, GaP, and InP. Large, state-of-the-art single crystals of GaAs, GaP, and InP have been grown by a VGF-based process, producing seeded 50 mm crystals with lower dislocation densities than crystals grown by alternative methods, including 750-gram ⟨111⟩-seeded InP boules with exceptional radial uniformity and silicon-doped ⟨100⟩ GaAs with dislocation densities below 300/cm² without requiring high dopant levels. These competing methods generally sacrifice some of LEC's flexibility (in-situ synthesis, larger boule lengths) in exchange for reduced thermal stress and defect density.

## 9. Industrial Significance

GaAs crystals are industrially produced by LEC with diameters of 150 mm, and LEC growth of 200 mm diameter GaAs crystals has already been demonstrated, underscoring the method's continued industrial relevance despite competition from Bridgman/VGF techniques, particularly where large-diameter, high-throughput boule production is required.
