# China's Export Controls on Critical Raw Materials and Rare Earth Elements

*A technical and geopolitical assessment*
*Prepared July 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

China has, over the past three years, transformed export licensing for rare earth elements (REEs) and a cluster of adjacent critical minerals (gallium, germanium, antimony, graphite, tungsten, and others) from a largely commercial/environmental regulatory matter into a formal instrument of geopolitical statecraft. This shift rests on a legal architecture built since 2020 — the Export Control Law (ECL), the 2024 Regulations on Export Control of Dual-Use Items, and a rapid cadence of Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM) announcements culminating in the October 2025 measures that, for the first time, asserted **extraterritorial jurisdiction** over foreign-made products containing Chinese-origin rare earth content. China controls roughly 69–70% of global rare earth mine output, an estimated 85–92% of global refining/separation capacity, and close to 90% of permanent magnet manufacturing — concentration levels far higher than for almost any other strategic commodity. This report traces the legal framework, historical evolution (2010–2026), the economic and price effects of specific control episodes (gallium, germanium, antimony, rare earths), the technological/defense exposure this creates for the United States, Japan, and the EU, and the state of Western diversification efforts as of mid-2026, including the fragile one-year Busan truce reached between Washington and Beijing in October 2025.

## 1. Legal and Institutional Framework

### 1.1 Foundational statute: the Export Control Law (2020)

China's modern control regime is anchored in the **Export Control Law (ECL)**, adopted by the National People's Congress Standing Committee in October 2020 and effective December 1, 2020. The ECL is a framework statute — analogous in structure to the U.S. Export Administration Regulations (EAR) — that establishes:

- A general statutory basis for regulating **dual-use items** (civilian/military applicability), military products, nuclear materials, and other goods, technologies, and services implicating national security or non-proliferation obligations.
- A **control list system**, administered principally by MOFCOM, permitting the government to designate specific items subject to licensing.
- An **Unreliable Entity List (UEL)** and export-prohibition mechanisms allowing MOFCOM to blacklist specific foreign entities.
- A statutory hook for **extraterritorial application**, present in nascent form from the outset but not substantively exercised until 2025.

### 1.2 The 2024 Regulations on Export Control of Dual-Use Items

Effective December 1, 2024, these regulations consolidated and operationalized the ECL's general principles into implementable rules. Critically, **Article 49** of the Regulations creates the legal basis for extraterritorial jurisdiction "under specified conditions" — the provision MOFCOM would later invoke to justify its most aggressive 2025 measures. Legal commentary frames the "territory + nationality" jurisdictional principle codified here as explicitly modeled on the logic of the U.S. Foreign Direct Product Rule (FDPR) and *de minimis* thresholds under EAR §734.3.

### 1.3 The 2025 escalation: Announcements No. 55–58, 61, and 62

On **October 9, 2025**, MOFCOM issued six major notices that represent the most consequential expansion of the regime to date:

| Notice | Core content |
|---|---|
| **No. 61** | Landmark extraterritorial measure: requires foreign entities to obtain MOFCOM licenses to re-export controlled rare earth elements (and downstream products incorporating them) between two non-China jurisdictions — a direct analogue to the U.S. FDPR/re-export concept |
| **No. 62** | Companion measure introducing China's own **"50% Rule"** (an "Affiliates Rule"), mirroring the U.S. Bureau of Industry and Security's ownership-threshold rule, and formally codifying the "territory + nationality" jurisdictional principle |
| **No. 58** | Export controls on lithium-battery and synthetic graphite anode materials, including high-performance batteries and associated manufacturing equipment/technology |
| **Others (55–57)** | Controls on semiconductor-related and superhard-material items |

A distinguishing technical feature of the rare earth-specific rules is a bifurcated **content threshold** modeled on, but distinct from, the U.S. de minimis rule:

- **0% threshold** for products directly containing controlled **heavy rare earth elements (HREEs)** — any detectable amount triggers a licensing requirement, regardless of quantity.
- **0.1% value threshold** for products that do not directly contain HREEs but incorporate permanent magnets or "target materials" manufactured from them — if Chinese-origin HREE content constitutes 0.1% or more of the finished component's value, a license is required.

Implementation was phased: the extraterritorial re-export controls (Notice 61's technology/equipment elements) took effect October 9, 2025, while the goods-based thresholds carried a grace period until December 1, 2025. In parallel, MOFCOM added 14 entities — mostly U.S. defense- and intelligence-linked organizations — to the Unreliable Entity List.

### 1.4 Licensing administration in practice

Since the April 2025 rare-earth controls, MOFCOM has demonstrated markedly tighter licensing scrutiny: longer approval timelines, more case-by-case review, and increased customs enforcement (higher case counts and penalty severity for undeclared or misrouted exports). European manufacturers in particular reported material delays through late 2025.

## 2. Historical Development

### 2.1 Precedent: the 2010 Japan embargo

The single most cited precedent for "weaponized" rare earth policy is the **September–November 2010 episode** tied to the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands dispute. After a Chinese trawler collided with Japanese Coast Guard vessels and its captain was detained, Chinese customs agents — according to contemporaneous reporting from multiple national customs administrations — halted REE shipments to Japan for roughly two months. At the time, China supplied an estimated **80–90% of Japan's rare earth demand** and controlled roughly 90% of world supply. Global rare earth prices rose roughly **tenfold over the following year**, though scholarship is divided on how much of that spike reflects a *targeted* embargo versus a coincident, pre-existing Chinese decision to tighten worldwide export quotas for environmental and industrial-consolidation reasons. A 2014 WTO ruling against China's rare earth export quota regime (brought by the U.S., EU, and Japan) contributed to price stabilization and catalyzed the first wave of Japanese and Western diversification efforts (e.g., Japan's stockpiling program and investment in Lynas Rare Earths).

### 2.2 2023: the opening moves of the current cycle

- **July–August 2023**: China imposed licensing requirements on **gallium and germanium** exports, citing "national security." Chinese customs data show the immediate effect: monthly gallium exports fell from roughly 6,876 kg (July 2023) to 227 kg (October 2023); germanium exports fell from 7,965 kg to 590 kg over the same window. Gallium prices in Rotterdam roughly doubled within months.
- Restrictions on **graphite** and certain rare-earth processing technologies followed later in 2023, extending controls from raw materials to know-how.

### 2.3 2024: escalation and explicit U.S. targeting

- **August 15, 2024**: Export limits on **antimony** announced (effective September 15). Chinese antimony exports subsequently fell by approximately **97%**, and antimony trioxide prices roughly doubled.
- **December 3, 2024**: MOFCOM issued a **blanket prohibition, in principle, on exports of gallium, germanium, antimony, and superhard materials to the United States** specifically — escalating from licensing requirements to an outright country-specific ban. This came directly in response to expanded U.S. semiconductor export controls announced days earlier.

### 2.4 2025: rare earths enter the arena directly

- **February 4, 2025**: Controls added on five further minerals, including tungsten, tellurium, bismuth, indium, and molybdenum, in response to the opening rounds of the U.S.–China tariff escalation under the second Trump administration.
- **April 4, 2025**: In direct response to the Trump administration's 54% tariff announcement two days earlier, MOFCOM imposed licensing controls on **seven rare earth elements and rare-earth magnets** — the first time REEs proper (rather than gallium/germanium/antimony, which are not classified as REEs) were directly weaponized in this cycle.
- **October 9, 2025**: The extraterritorial Notices 61/62 described in Section 1.3 — the most sweeping measure yet, extending China's reach to foreign-made goods manufactured anywhere in the world using Chinese-origin rare earth content or technology.
- **Japan-specific escalation (late 2025)**: Amid a separate deterioration in China–Japan relations over Taiwan-related statements by Japan's government, China applied REE and broader dual-use export restrictions explicitly justified on foreign-policy/sovereignty grounds — a notable shift from the trade-retaliation framing used against the U.S., and one CSIS analysts describe as the closest analogue yet to the 2010 precedent.
- **October 30, 2025 (Busan)**: At an APEC-sidelines summit, Presidents Trump and Xi agreed to a **one-year truce**: China suspended implementation of the October 9 extraterritorial measures (rolling the regime back to the April 4, 2025 baseline, which itself remains in force for the seven controlled REEs) and the standing gallium/germanium/antimony ban against the U.S. (through November 27, 2026), in exchange for U.S. tariff reductions and suspension of the BIS 50% Affiliates Rule.
- **December 2025–mid-2026**: MOFCOM reported issuing "general licenses" for civilian rare-earth exports under the truce; EU officials estimated approval rates rising from roughly 50% (October 2025) to roughly 75% (December 2025). Independent trade-data analysis (Global Trade Alert) subsequently examined November 2025–April 2026 customs data to assess whether actual export *volumes* recovered to pre-crisis norms — a distinct question from whether the licensing *process* eased, since the underlying April 2025 licensing regime for the seven controlled elements remains fully in force.

**Interpretation.** Analysts broadly characterize the Busan truce as a **tactical pause, not a structural rollback**: it removes the newest and most extraterritorially aggressive layer of control while leaving intact the licensing regime for the seven most defense-relevant rare earths and China's underlying legal capacity (Article 49, Notices 61/62) to reimpose or expand controls at will when the one-year suspension lapses.

## 3. Geopolitical Context

### 3.1 Why rare earths and critical minerals became a lever

China's leverage rests on three distinct forms of concentration, each more extreme than the last:

1. **Mining**: ~69–70% of global rare earth mine production (2024–2025, USGS).
2. **Refining/separation**: an estimated 85–92% of global rare earth *processing* capacity — a chokepoint far more concentrated than mining, since raw ore from Australia, Myanmar, or the U.S. is frequently shipped to China for separation regardless of where it is mined.
3. **Magnet manufacturing**: roughly 90% of high-performance permanent magnet (NdFeB, SmCo) production.

This layered concentration means diversifying *mining* alone — the easiest link to replicate — does little to reduce dependency unless matched by comparably difficult investments in separation chemistry and magnet metallurgy, both of which China built through decades of state support, lower historical environmental standards, and scale economics that undercut Western producers (notably contributing to the shutdown of the U.S. Mountain Pass mine's downstream operations in the 1990s–2000s before its 2017 revival).

### 3.2 Escalation logic: from trade retaliation to sovereignty signaling

Through 2024 and early-to-mid 2025, Chinese controls were consistently framed as **calibrated, proportionate responses** to specific U.S. actions — chiefly the expanding scope of U.S. semiconductor export controls (December 2024) and tariff escalation under the second Trump administration (February and April 2025). CSIS analysis characterizes the late-2025 measures against Japan as qualitatively different: explicitly tied to *foreign-policy and sovereignty* signaling (Taiwan-related statements by Japanese officials) rather than trade retaliation, evoking the 2010 precedent more directly than any intervening episode. This suggests Beijing is willing to deploy the same legal machinery for both economic-coercion and territorial/security-signaling purposes, widening the range of triggers that could prompt future restrictions.

### 3.3 The extraterritoriality precedent

Notices 61 and 62 represent a doctrinal shift with consequences beyond the immediate mineral categories covered. By asserting jurisdiction over re-exports between two non-Chinese jurisdictions and introducing an "Affiliates Rule" mirroring BIS's 50% ownership threshold, China has signaled it can construct a **mirror-image FDPR regime** applicable to any input where it holds comparable market dominance (e.g., battery materials, superhard materials, potentially future categories like certain semiconductor-adjacent chemicals). Legal commentators note this was explicitly framed as a direct response to the U.S. Affiliates Rule, illustrating an emerging pattern of **tit-for-tat regulatory mimicry** between the two systems.

### 3.4 Allied and third-country responses

- **Japan**: Having been the primary target in 2010, Japan pursued the most mature diversification strategy of any consumer nation — government stockpiling, direct equity investment in Lynas Rare Earths (Australia), magnet recycling, and material-substitution R&D (e.g., reduced-dysprosium magnet designs at Toyota and other manufacturers). Despite this, the 2025 controls tied to bilateral tensions demonstrate residual exposure.
- **European Union**: Reported some of the longest licensing delays under the tightened 2025 regime; the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act (2024) sets domestic extraction, processing, and recycling benchmarks partly in response to this exposure.
- **United States**: Moved from licensing-based countermeasures (BIS Entity List actions, the Affiliates Rule) toward direct industrial policy — equity stakes, price floors, and offtake guarantees for domestic producers (detailed in Section 5).

### 4.2 Price effects of specific control episodes

- **Antimony**: Following the August 2024 restrictions, prices rose roughly **250% over 2024**, reaching all-time highs of $39,500–$40,000/tonne in Rotterdam by year-end, with traders citing "no large alternative source" to pivot to.
- **Gallium**: Rotterdam gallium (99.99% min) prices rose from roughly **$250–265/kg just before the June 2023 controls announcement to $500–600/kg by December 2025** — a swing exceeding 100% at points, with substantial volatility in between tied to licensing bottlenecks rather than underlying scarcity of ore.
- **Gallium/germanium export volumes**: Chinese monthly exports of both fell by roughly 90%+ in the months immediately following the 2023 controls (gallium: 6,876 kg → 227 kg between July and October 2023; germanium: 7,965 kg → 590 kg over the same period), before partially recovering over subsequent quarters — but remaining well below pre-restriction baselines even a year later.

### 4.3 Modeled macroeconomic impact (United States)

A USGS-published quantitative model (Nassar et al., 2024) estimated that a **complete Chinese restriction of net gallium and germanium exports** to the U.S. could reduce U.S. GDP by:

- **$3.1 billion** (range: $1.7B–$8.2B) from gallium alone
- **$0.4 billion** (range: $0.01B–$1.1B) from germanium alone
- **$3.4 billion** (range: $1.7B–$9.0B) if both were disrupted simultaneously

These are modest relative to U.S. GDP (~$29 trillion) in aggregate, but the model captures only direct/indirect input-output effects, not downstream disruption to specific high-value defense or semiconductor programs, nor dynamic effects such as delayed capital investment or lost competitiveness in gallium-arsenide-dependent segments (power electronics, RF devices, optoelectronics).

### 4.4 Structural economic vulnerabilities beyond price

Analysts highlight a **"by-product trap"**: gallium and germanium are typically recovered as by-products of primary aluminum (bauxite) and zinc/coal processing respectively, meaning supply is largely unresponsive to price signals in the short-to-medium run — new gallium supply cannot simply be "mined" faster in response to high prices, since it depends on unrelated primary-metal production decisions. This structural rigidity compounds the market-power effect of China's dominant refining position and explains why price spikes from 2023–2025 controls have been sharp and durable rather than quickly arbitraged away by new entrants.

### 4.5 Sectoral exposure

- **Electric vehicles**: Battery-grade graphite and NdFeB magnets for EV motors are both subject to control; automakers have publicly flagged the difficulty of excluding Chinese content from graphite supply chains on short notice, complicating compliance with U.S. tax-credit eligibility rules tied to "foreign entity of concern" sourcing restrictions.
- **Semiconductors**: Gallium (GaAs substrates) underpins high-frequency/high-power RF devices and compound semiconductors used in 5G infrastructure, defense radar, and power electronics — segments with few viable near-term substitutes.
- **Renewable energy**: Wind turbine permanent-magnet generators (particularly offshore direct-drive designs) are NdFeB-intensive and were an early flashpoint during the 2010–2011 price spike.

## 5. Technological and Defense Consequences

### 5.1 Direct materiel exposure

Rare earth magnets are embedded, often in small but mission-critical quantities, throughout advanced weapons platforms:

- A **Virginia-class submarine** contains an estimated **~9,200 pounds** of rare earth materials.
- An **F-35 fighter** requires more than **900 pounds** of rare earths, and uses roughly **50 pounds of samarium-cobalt magnets per aircraft** (Lockheed Martin is the principal U.S. consumer of samarium for this application). SmCo magnets are specifically valued for retaining magnetism at extreme temperatures.
- The U.S. currently has **no domestic heavy rare earth (HREE) separation capacity** — dysprosium and terbium, both essential for high-temperature-tolerant magnets used in jet engines and precision-guided munitions, remain almost entirely dependent on Chinese (or Chinese-linked Malaysian) processing even where mined elsewhere.
- A 2025 Government Accountability Office estimate put the cost of closing U.S. defense-sector rare earth supply gaps at roughly **$18.5 billion**.

### 5.2 The refining/finished-component gap

A recurring theme in defense-sector analysis is that stockpiling raw ore or oxide does little to solve near-term readiness problems: defense contractors need **finished or near-finished materials** — specifically sintered NdFeB and SmCo magnets machined to specification — not unrefined feedstock. This distinction matters because most current U.S. and allied investment (Section 5.3) targets midstream separation and magnet fabrication, which take years to scale, rather than simply expanding mine output, which can in principle be increased faster.

### 5.3 Western and allied diversification efforts (state as of mid-2026)

| Initiative | Details |
|---|---|
| **MP Materials (Mountain Pass, CA / Fort Worth, TX)** | July 2025: entered a multibillion-dollar public-private partnership with the U.S. DoD, including a $400M equity investment plus $150M loan, DoD warrants for up to a 15% equity stake (making DoD potentially MP's largest shareholder), a decade-long price-floor and offtake commitment for NdPr products, and DoD commitment to purchase all magnet output from the new "10X" facility. MP has committed to **cease sales to China** and prioritize domestic/ex-China supply chains. Nd/Pr magnet capacity is targeted to rise from ~3,000 t/yr to ~10,000 t/yr by 2028. ### 5.4 Assessment of diversification timelines

Even optimistic industry and defense-analyst assessments place full-spectrum Western supply chain independence — spanning mining, separation (particularly for HREEs), and finished-magnet production at defense-grade specifications — at **5–10 years out**, given the multi-year lead times for permitting, environmental compliance, and process qualification in defense applications. Mining analysts note that Beijing's underlying legal and market position — roughly 85–92% of global refining and ~90% of magnet manufacturing — is not meaningfully diminished by the Busan truce or by current Western investment levels, meaning short- and medium-term supply risk for the most defense-critical heavy rare earths (dysprosium, terbium, samarium) persists regardless of the trade truce's headline outcome.

## 6. Synthesis: Structural Assessment

Several patterns emerge from the 2023–2026 period that are likely to persist beyond the current one-year truce:

1. **Legal infrastructure now exists for indefinite escalation.** The ECL, the 2024 Regulations, and Article 49 in particular give MOFCOM standing authority to reimpose or expand controls — including extraterritorial re-export controls and ownership-based "affiliates" rules — without new primary legislation. The October 2025 measures function as a demonstrated capability, not merely a one-off response; they can be reactivated at low institutional cost when the Busan suspension lapses (October/November 2026).

2. **Concentration is layered, not singular.** Mining diversification (Australia, U.S., Myanmar) has genuinely progressed since 2010, but refining and magnet-manufacturing concentration remain close to their historical peaks, meaning the "choke point" has migrated downstream faster than Western capital has followed it.

3. **The coercion rationale is broadening.** Early controls (2023–2024) were framed as trade retaliation tied to specific U.S. actions. By late 2025, China applied the same legal machinery to a sovereignty/foreign-policy dispute with Japan, suggesting the toolkit's use case is expanding beyond bilateral U.S.–China trade friction.

4. **Western responses are converging on state-directed industrial policy** — equity stakes, price floors, and guaranteed offtake — rather than pure market mechanisms, reflecting an assessment that private capital alone will not close the gap given China's decades-long head start and by-product/scale economics.

5. **The Busan truce is best read as a tactical pause.** It rolls back the newest and most extraterritorially aggressive layer (October 2025 rules) while leaving intact the April 2025 licensing regime for the seven most sensitive rare earths — a hedge that preserves Chinese leverage while defusing the most acute crisis.
