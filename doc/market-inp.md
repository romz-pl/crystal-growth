## 1. What InP Is and Why It Matters

Indium phosphide is a III-V direct-bandgap compound semiconductor. Its high electron mobility and direct bandgap (~1.35 eV, corresponding to the 1310–1550 nm telecom windows) make it the substrate of choice for high-speed, low-power optoelectronic devices — laser diodes, electro-absorption modulators, photodetectors, and increasingly full photonic integrated circuits (PICs). Unlike SiC, InP is melt-grown, but phosphorus's high vapor pressure at melting temperature makes crystal growth technically difficult and low-yield compared with silicon or even GaAs.

## 2. Value Chain

**Upstream — raw materials**
- **Indium**: a byproduct of zinc refining; China supplies roughly 70% of global primary indium output (USGS estimate).
- **Phosphorus** and high-purity synthesis into **polycrystalline InP** feedstock — a distinct upstream step. Specialist polycrystal suppliers (e.g., China's JM Gallium) feed crystal growers rather than growing crystals themselves.

**Midstream — single-crystal growth**
Two dominant melt-growth methods:
- **Liquid Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC)**: crystal pulled upward through a B₂O₃ encapsulant layer that suppresses phosphorus evaporation; historically the higher-volume method but produces higher dislocation density.
- **Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF) / Vertical Bridgman (VB)**: melt solidified in a stationary crucible via a controlled axial temperature gradient, without crystal rotation. Produces markedly lower dislocation density and better electrical/doping uniformity, and has become the preferred method — VGF-grown bulk material is estimated at over half of the wafer market by volume, and its share is growing. VGF-grown bulk wafers accounted for 54.98% of the indium phosphide wafer market size in 2025. Some producers now combine VGF and VB stages (VGF-VB) specifically to push diameters past 4 inches while controlling twinning and dislocation clusters — InP has the lowest stacking-fault energy of any common III-V, making it intrinsically more prone to twinning and dislocations than GaAs.

**Downstream — wafering and finishing**
Ingots are sliced, lapped, and chemo-mechanically polished to epi-ready (angstrom-level roughness) surfaces, then sold either as bare substrates or as epitaxial wafers (with device-layer epitaxy, typically by MOCVD or MBE, already grown on top — commanding the highest margins in the chain).

**End use**: optical transceivers/PICs for telecom and datacom (dominant application), LiDAR, high-frequency HBTs/terahertz devices, satellite communications, quantum photonics, and (a smaller niche) high-efficiency solar cells.

## 4. Production Capacity — The Central 2026 Story

This market is currently defined by an acute **supply-demand imbalance**, driven by the AI data-center optics boom colliding with a geopolitical supply shock.

- Global effective production capacity is estimated at only 600,000 to 750,000 wafers annually, while demand reaches 2.6 to 3.0 million wafers, creating a supply gap exceeding 70% (2026 estimate, per Chinese industry data reported by BigGo Finance — treat as directional rather than precise).
- On **February 4, 2025**, China's Ministry of Commerce and General Administration of Customs added indium phosphide substrates to its export-control list, part of a broader package covering tungsten, tellurium, bismuth, molybdenum and indium, issued amid the wider US-China trade dispute. This required Tongmei (AXT's Chinese manufacturing subsidiary) to seek permits from China's Ministry of Commerce before it could export gallium arsenide, germanium, and (from February 2025) indium phosphide substrates.
- The permitting friction directly hit revenue: AXT cut its Q4 2025 revenue guidance to $22.5–23.5 million, primarily due to fewer export control permits for indium phosphide being issued by China's Ministry of Commerce than previously expected. Permits have since been issued selectively — Tongmei received export permits in August 2025 to resume shipping indium phosphide substrates to certain additional customers — but the process remains slow and unpredictable.
- The disruption pushed customers of AXT/Tongmei substrates, including Taiwanese epi/device makers, into shortage: Lumentum is sold out through 2028 despite quadrupling output, while Taiwanese optical products makers VPEC and LandMark Optoelectronics faced InP substrate disruptions from AXT permit delays.
- This drove a major capital event: in April 2026, AXT closed an underwritten public offering of 8,560,311 shares at $64.25 per share, with total gross proceeds of around $550 million, plus a subsequent overallotment raising roughly $82.5 million more — combining to a capital raise of $632.5 million in support of Tongmei's indium phosphide capacity expansion as well as R&D investment in new products like 6-inch indium phosphide. AXT's stock moved dramatically on the news — the share price rose over 5,000 percent compared to a year earlier ($1.23), reaching $69.24 at time of that report.
- AXT is also locking in demand: on June 25, 2026, AXT-Tongmei entered a three-year Master Development and Supply Agreement with Coherent Corp. to develop and mass-supply 6-inch indium phosphide wafer substrates, with AXT expanding Beijing manufacturing capacity from 2026 through 2028 and Coherent making a prepayment of about $22.3 million against future purchases.
- Domestic Chinese substrate makers are scaling fast to capture business that can't clear export permitting: Yunnan Germanium announced a 189 million yuan (~$28 million) investment in April 2026 to expand production capacity to 450,000 InP wafers annually, with 2025 shipments up 74% year-on-year; Guangdong Xiandao launched a new project via its Guangdong Xianrui subsidiary targeting 40 tons/year of InP crystal feedstock. Even so, domestic Chinese production of high-end 6-inch substrates remains under 5% of that segment — the technology gap at the largest, most advanced diameter is still wide.
- The friction is spreading beyond InP itself: although indium metal is not yet formally export-controlled, buyers report growing customs scrutiny, including a European buyer required for the first time this year to disclose end-user information including locations.
- Political visibility: the shortage prompted Coherent's CEO to travel to Beijing alongside a US presidential delegation in May 2026 to press the issue.

## 5. Wafer Sizes and Technology Trend

The industry is pushing toward larger diameters to cut cost-per-device:
- Standard sizes: 2-inch (50.8 mm) and 3-inch legacy; 4-inch now mainstream; **6-inch is the current frontier**, actively being industrialized by AXT/Tongmei (with Coherent as anchor customer) and targeted by Sumitomo's R&D.
- Beijing Tongmei Xtal Technology completed a manufacturing expansion project in February 2025, doubling its production capacity for four-inch and six-inch indium phosphide substrates.
- The transition to larger substrates enhances high-speed interconnect viability, with increased surface area allowing die output over 150% greater than traditional two-inch wafers.
- A parallel technical trend is **InP-on-Si** hybrid integration — bonding or growing InP device layers on silicon substrates to leverage CMOS-scale processing economics — which industry trackers flag as the fastest-growing (if still niche) sub-segment.

## 6. Pricing

Reported 2026 spot pricing reflects the acute shortage:
- 2-inch optical-communication-grade InP substrates rose from about $800/wafer in early 2025 to $2,300–2,500/wafer by April 2026 — roughly a 200% increase — with spot prices for urgent orders exceeding $3,000.
- High-end 6-inch substrate prices climbed from roughly $1,400 to about $5,000 over the same period — an increase of over 250%.
- Analysts cited in that reporting expect the price boom to persist through 2026–2027, contingent on how quickly new capacity (both AXT/Tongmei's expansion and Chinese domestic entrants) comes online and how export permitting evolves.

## 7. Applications

- **Optical transceivers / datacom-telecom** — by far the largest end use, and the current growth engine, driven by hyperscale data-center buildouts moving to 800G and 1.6T optical interconnects for AI compute clusters, plus ongoing 5G/6G backhaul.
- **Photonic integrated circuits (PICs)** — coherent optical modules, EMLs, Mach-Zehnder modulators, high-speed photodetectors.
- **High-frequency electronics** — HBTs and terahertz devices (e.g., radar), exploiting InP's high electron mobility.
- **LiDAR** — automotive and industrial sensing.
- **Satellite communications** and defense/aerospace.
- **Quantum photonics** — an emerging, government-funded niche; e.g., EU-funded work has demonstrated room-temperature InP quantum-dot lasers, and the US CHIPS Act has directed dedicated funding toward domestic InP capacity for quantum applications.
- **Solar cells** — high-efficiency multi-junction cells, a smaller volume application.

## 8. Key Trends Shaping the Market (2026)

1. **AI-driven demand shock**: hyperscaler optical-interconnect buildout is the single biggest demand driver, arguably outpacing telecom's traditional role.
2. **Geopolitical supply risk is now structural**, not transient: China's February 2025 export controls on InP (alongside tungsten, tellurium, bismuth, molybdenum, indium) have made permit-timing a material line-item risk for Western manufacturers, evidenced directly in AXT's guidance cuts.
3. **Capacity race**: AXT/Tongmei's ~$630M raise, Coherent's own epi-capacity expansion, and rapid scale-up by second-tier Chinese domestic substrate makers (Yunnan Germanium, Guangdong Xiandao) are the main capacity responses; full relief is not expected before 2027–2028 given multi-year qualification cycles with optical-module OEMs.
4. **Diameter scaling** (4-inch → 6-inch) as the core cost-reduction lever, alongside InP-on-Si hybrid integration as a longer-horizon path to CMOS-scale economics.
5. **Reshoring/diversification pressure** in the US and EU, though genuine non-Asian, non-Chinese-linked capacity remains limited (Freiberger in Germany is the main Western alternative outside AXT's China-based manufacturing).
