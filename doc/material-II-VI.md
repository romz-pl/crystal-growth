# II–VI Compound Semiconductors Grown from the Melt

This is a systematic list of II–VI binary and ternary compounds for which **melt growth** (as opposed to vapor-phase or solution/low-temperature growth) has been reported or is practiced. For compounds with high dissociation pressure at the melting point, "melt growth" typically means growth under an applied inert-gas overpressure (High-Pressure Bridgman) or under liquid encapsulation.

**Bold** entries mark the favorite / most widely used method for that compound.

## Zinc chalcogenides

| Compound | Melting point | Melt-growth methods reported | Favorite method |
|---|---|---|---|
| ZnS | ~1830 °C (under pressure; sublimes at 1 atm) | High-Pressure Bridgman (HPB); High-Pressure Vertical Zone Melting (HPVZM) | **High-Pressure Bridgman (HPB)** |
| ZnSe | 1525 °C | High-Pressure Bridgman (HPB); High-Pressure Vertical Bridgman (HPVB); Czochralski (rare, under encapsulation) | **High-Pressure Vertical Bridgman (HPVB)** |
| ZnTe | 1295 °C | Vertical Bridgman; High-Pressure Bridgman; Horizontal Bridgman | **Vertical Bridgman** |

## Notes on methodology

- **High-Pressure (Vertical) Bridgman — HPB/HPVB**: the dominant technique for the "high-vapor-pressure" II–VI chalcogenides (ZnS, ZnSe, CdS, CdSe), carried out under 10–100 atm inert gas overpressure to suppress dissociation/volatilization at the melting point. This approach was pioneered for II–VI compound growth in the late 1960s and remains the most widespread method for this materials class.
- **Vertical Bridgman / VGF**: the workhorse for CdTe and CdZnTe (CZT), valued for ease of instrumentation and interface-curvature control via top/bottom gradient tuning, though wall contact with the ampoule can raise stress and defect density — a limitation addressed by detached-solidification variants.
- **Traveling Heater Method (THM)**: a solution-growth-through-a-molten-zone technique operated at lower temperature than pure melt growth; used preferentially for CdTe, HgCdTe, and PbTe where it yields lower defect densities (fewer low-angle grain boundaries) than straight Bridgman growth, at the cost of slower growth rates.
- Several nominally "II–VI" wide-gap materials (MgS, MgSe, MgTe) are not practically bulk melt-grown as standalone binaries; in the crystal-growth literature they appear almost exclusively as constituents of melt- or vapor-grown ternary/quaternary alloys (e.g., (Cd,Mg)Te, (Cd,Zn,Mg)Te) or as MBE-grown epitaxial layers.
- Vapor-phase methods (PVT, Markov method, seeded physical vapor transport) are the leading *alternative* to melt growth for ZnSe, ZnTe, and ZnS crystals intended for optical-grade or low-defect-density applications, and are noted here only for contrast — they are excluded from the "melt-grown" tables above.
