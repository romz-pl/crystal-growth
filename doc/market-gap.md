# Global Commercial Gallium Phosphide (GaP) Single-Crystal Market

## 1. Executive Summary

Gallium phosphide (GaP) is a mature, niche III-V compound semiconductor material occupying a small but persistent corner of the compound-semiconductor substrate industry. Compared with its higher-volume siblings gallium arsenide (GaAs) and indium phosphide (InP), the GaP single-crystal market is small in absolute dollar terms — independent market-research estimates put current global revenue in the range of roughly **$100–150 million**, with most forecasts projecting mid-to-high single-digit or low double-digit CAGR growth through the early 2030s. The market is fragmented, dominated by second-tier compound-semiconductor specialists (mostly Chinese and a handful of Western/Japanese suppliers) rather than the "big three" that dominate GaAs (Sumitomo Electric, Freiberger, AXT/Beijing Tongmei), because GaP volumes are too small to attract the largest players' primary strategic focus. The value chain runs from high-purity gallium and red-phosphorus feedstock, through polycrystalline GaP synthesis, single-crystal boule growth (overwhelmingly via the Liquid Encapsulated Czochralski, LEC, method), to wafer slicing, lapping, polishing and epi-ready finishing, feeding downstream LED, optoelectronic, and terahertz/photonic-instrumentation manufacturers.

## 2. Material Background

- **Composition/structure:** GaP is a III–V compound semiconductor with the zinc-blende (cubic) crystal structure, lattice constant ≈ 0.545 nm.
- **Band gap:** Indirect band gap of ≈ 2.26 eV (300 K) — one of the widest indirect gaps among common III-V binaries, giving GaP its characteristic pale orange/yellow, semi-translucent appearance.
- **Electrical properties:** Electron mobility ≈ 250 cm²/V·s, hole mobility ≈ 100–150 cm²/V·s (values vary by source and doping); can be doped n-type (S, Te, Si) or p-type (Zn) or grown semi-insulating/undoped for high-resistivity applications.
- **Optical properties:** Because of the indirect gap, GaP LEDs historically required nitrogen or ZnO isoelectronic doping to achieve usable luminescence efficiency for red/orange/green emission — a technology largely superseded, but the substrate itself remains valuable for its transparency and nonlinear-optical/terahertz properties.

## 3. Value Chain

### 3.1 Upstream feedstock
- **High-purity gallium metal** (6N–7N, i.e., 99.9999–99.99999%) — predominantly refined in China, which historically accounts for roughly 90%+ of global primary gallium refining capacity (a by-product of bauxite/alumina and zinc processing).
- **High-purity red/white phosphorus** — sourced from specialty chemical suppliers.
- **Synthesis into polycrystalline GaP** — Ga and P are combined under high pressure (owing to phosphorus's high vapor pressure at GaP's ~1465 °C melting point) in sealed or horizontal-Bridgman-type synthesis reactors to produce polycrystalline GaP feed ("polycrystal") for subsequent single-crystal growth. Some suppliers (e.g., JM Gallium in China, active mainly in InP but illustrative of the model) exemplify the specialist-polycrystal-feedstock tier that many single-crystal growers depend on rather than self-synthesizing.

### 3.2 Single-crystal growth
- **Liquid Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC)** is overwhelmingly the dominant commercial growth technique for GaP, just as it historically was for GaAs before VGF/VB displaced it in that material. A B₂O₃ (boric oxide) encapsulant layer floats on the melt inside a pyrolytic boron nitride (pBN) crucible under high inert-gas pressure, suppressing phosphorus evaporation while the seed crystal is pulled and rotated. Multi-heater, computer-controlled diameter and thermal-gradient control systems (of the type Freiberger and others use for GaAs) are directly applicable to GaP growth.
- **Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF) / Vertical Bridgman (VB)** are used by some suppliers for GaP, offering lower dislocation density and reduced thermal stress versus LEC, mirroring the trend seen in the InP and GaAs markets, though LEC remains the reference/default process for GaP because of established process know-how and lower capital intensity for the modest wafer volumes involved.
- **Boule diameters:** Commercial GaP boules are almost universally grown and sliced at **2-inch (50 mm)** diameter, with **3-inch (75 mm)** available from several suppliers. Larger diameters (4–6 inch) are technically demonstrated in the wider compound-semiconductor wafer industry but are not standard commercial GaP offerings; industry sources note that wafers above 6 inch are "more challenging to produce" for this material system, reflecting the far lower R&D and capex investment GaP has received relative to GaAs/InP scaling efforts.

### 3.3 Wafer fabrication
Standard finishing steps mirror other III-V wafering: ingot orientation/X-ray alignment → wire-saw slicing → edge-rounding → lapping → etching → chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP) → optional "epi-ready" surface preparation for LPE, MOCVD, or MBE overgrowth. Wafers are offered:
- **Doping:** n-type (S- or Te-doped), p-type (Zn-doped), semi-insulating/undoped (high resistivity, e.g., ~10¹⁴ Ω·cm, used in THz applications)
- **Orientation:** predominantly ⟨100⟩ and ⟨111⟩
- **Surface finish:** as-cut, lapped, etched, single-side polished (SSP), double-side polished (DSP), epi-ready
- **Thickness:** typically 0.3–0.5 mm for 2–3 inch wafers

## 6. Production Capacity and Wafer Sizes

- Commercial GaP wafers are standardized at **2-inch (50 mm)** and **3-inch (75 mm)** diameters; these two sizes cover the overwhelming majority of supplier catalogs surveyed.
- Larger-diameter GaP (4–6 inch) is referenced in passing by some industry sources as technically achievable but is not a standard commercial product, unlike GaAs and InP, which have both scaled to 6-inch (150 mm) production at the leading suppliers (Freiberger, Sumitomo). This diameter gap reflects GaP's comparatively low investment priority: the R&D and capital that drove GaAs/InP diameter scaling (defect-density reduction, automation, AI-assisted process control at 150 mm) has not been similarly directed at GaP, whose addressable market does not justify the capex.

## 9. Key Market Trends

1. **Geopolitical/supply-chain risk from Chinese gallium export controls.** China refines the substantial majority of the world's primary gallium and, since August 2023, has imposed export licensing controls on gallium (and germanium) explicitly framed as a response to Western semiconductor-equipment export restrictions. A November 2025 bilateral agreement suspended the tightest controls for one year, with the suspension period running to a **27 November 2026 expiration**, after which restrictions could be reinstated; third-country transshipment (e.g., via Hong Kong or Singapore) remains prohibited even during the suspension. Because GaP synthesis requires the same high-purity gallium feedstock as GaAs and GaN, GaP producers face the same upstream exposure, though industry commentary (e.g., Digitimes' 2023 analysis of the GaAs/GaN supply chain) suggests the direct substrate-level impact is manageable because Chinese suppliers already dominate substrate-scale capacity expansion, and much of the higher-value epitaxial-layer gallium demand (via trimethylgallium for MOCVD) is sourced from Europe, the US, and Japan rather than China directly.
2. **Displacement in core lighting applications.** GaN/InGaN-based blue and white LEDs, and AlGaInP-on-GaAs red/amber LEDs, have captured the high-brightness/high-volume segments that GaP-based visible LEDs historically served, pushing GaP's commercial center of gravity toward lower-brightness indicator/backlight LEDs and non-lighting niches.
3. **Growth in niche photonics and THz instrumentation demand.** Semi-insulating, high-resistivity GaP (particularly (110)-oriented material) has carved out a durable niche in terahertz time-domain spectroscopy and nonlinear/electro-optic photonics, an application area cited by multiple specialty suppliers (Heeger Materials, UniversityWafer) as a current growth driver, distinct from the legacy LED business.
4. **Fragmented, non-vertically-integrated supply base.** Unlike GaAs/InP, no dominant 2–3 firm oligopoly has emerged in GaP; supply is spread across a long tail of mostly Chinese specialty crystal-growth firms plus a handful of Western/Japanese boutique and distributor-model suppliers, with limited independently verifiable capacity data.
5. **Wafer-size stagnation relative to GaAs/InP.** While GaAs and InP have scaled toward 150 mm (6-inch) production at leading suppliers with associated defect-density and automation investment, GaP remains largely confined to 2–3 inch commercial wafers, reflecting comparatively limited capital investment in the material.
6. **General industry direction toward automation/digital process control.** Broader compound-semiconductor crystal-growth trends — AI-assisted process simulation, automated diameter control, improved crucible/encapsulant materials — are gradually diffusing into adjacent, smaller-volume III-V materials like GaP as growth-equipment vendors (e.g., HF-Kejing) apply techniques developed for higher-volume GaAs/InP lines.
