# III–V Compound Semiconductors Grown from the Melt

Of the twelve zinc-blende III–V binaries (BP, BAs, BSb, AlP, AlAs, AlSb, GaP, GaAs, GaSb, InP, InAs, InSb), only a subset have congruent melting behavior, manageable dissociation vapor pressure, and thermodynamic stability suitable for practical **melt growth** (as opposed to vapor-phase or solution growth). The wurtzite nitrides (BN, AlN, GaN, InN) do not melt congruently at accessible pressures and are excluded — they are grown from vapor (HVPE, MOCVD, MBE), by ammonothermal/solvothermal methods, or under extreme high-pressure high-temperature (HPHT) conditions, not by conventional melt growth.

Below is the exhaustive list of III–V binaries industrially or experimentally grown from the melt, together with the growth methods used for each. Ternary/quaternary alloys (e.g., InGaAs, GaInSb, AlGaSb) are appended separately, since melt growth of these is well documented but compositionally more challenging (segregation).

## Binary III–V Compounds

| Material | Melting Point (°C) | Growth Methods Used | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GaAs** | 1238 | **Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC)**, Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF), Vertical Bridgman (VB), Horizontal Bridgman (HB) | Historically dominant via LEC (high dislocation density but large diameters); VGF/VB increasingly favored industrially for lower dislocation density and better uniformity |
| **InP** | 1062 | **Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF)**, Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC), Vertical Bridgman (VB) | High As/P dissociation pressure requires liquid encapsulation ($\text{B}_2 \text{O}_3$) or sealed ampoule; VGF preferred for low dislocation density substrates |
| **GaP** | 1465 | **Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC)**, Liquid-Encapsulated Vertical Gradient Freeze (LE-VGF) | High dissociation pressure and high melting point make growth challenging; LE-VGF reduces strain vs. LEC but has scale-up nucleation issues |
| **GaSb** | 712 | **Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF)**, Vertical Bridgman (VB), Czochralski (CZ) | Congruent melting, low dissociation pressure — one of the easier III–Vs to grow from melt; low melting point simplifies furnace design |
| **InAs** | 943 | **Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC)**, Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF), Vertical Bridgman (VB) | Moderate dissociation pressure; LEC and VGF both industrially used |
| **InSb** | 527 | **Czochralski (CZ)**, Liquid-Encapsulated Czochralski (LEC), Vertical Bridgman (VB), Vertical Gradient Freeze (VGF) | Low melting point and negligible dissociation pressure — no encapsulation strictly required; conventional CZ pulling is straightforward and widely used |
| **AlSb** | 1060 | Bridgman (VB/HB), Czochralski (CZ, encapsulated) | Highly reactive/hygroscopic melt ($\text{Al}_2 \text{O}_3$/moisture sensitivity); melt growth demonstrated but far less industrially mature than the above |
| **AlAs** | 1740 (peritectic behavior reported) | Bridgman (limited/experimental) | Extremely hygroscopic and prone to oxidation; rarely grown as bulk melt crystal — almost always grown epitaxially (MBE/MOCVD) instead; included for completeness |
| **AlP** | 2000 (approx., decomposes) | Not practically melt-grown | Decomposes before/at melting under normal pressure; only grown via flux or vapor methods in practice — listed for completeness of the III–V family, not as a real melt-growth candidate |
| **BP** | ~1100 (decomposes near melting) | Not practically melt-grown | Decomposes at/near its melting point at 1 atm; grown instead by chemical vapor transport or high-pressure solution methods |
| **BAs** | Decomposes before melting | Not melt-grown | No congruent melt exists at accessible conditions; grown by CVD/vapor transport |
| **BSb** | Decomposes before melting | Not melt-grown | Thermodynamically unstable as bulk material; no established melt growth |

## Ternary/Quaternary III–V Alloys Grown from the Melt

| Material | Growth Methods Used | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **InAsₓP₁₋ₓ** | **Vertical Gradient Freeze / Bridgman (with melt replenishment or supply)**, Traveling Heat Solvent | Compositional segregation is the central challenge; solute-feeding/melt-replenishment techniques developed to maintain uniform $x$ |
| **InₓGa₁₋ₓSb** | **Double-Crucible Czochralski**, Solute-Feeding Czochralski, Bridgman/Gradient Freeze | Double-crucible and solute-feeding CZ variants specifically developed to control alloy homogeneity along growth axis |
| **InAsₓP₁₋ₓ** | Bridgman, Gradient Freeze | Less mature than GaInSb/GaInAs; segregation control remains difficult |
| **AlₓGa₁₋ₓSb** | Bridgman, Gradient Freeze | Grown mainly for IR optoelectronic substrate applications |

## Summary of Favorite (Most Widely Used/Preferred) Methods

- **GaAs** → **LEC** (historically dominant; VGF gaining ground for low-defect applications)
- **InP** → **VGF**
- **GaP** → **LEC**
- **GaSb** → **VGF**
- **InAs** → **LEC**
- **InSb** → **CZ** (conventional, non-encapsulated)
- **AlSb** → Bridgman (no single dominant industrial favorite; niche material)
- **InₓGa₁₋ₓAs** → Bridgman/VGF with melt replenishment
- **InₓGa₁₋ₓSb** → Double-crucible CZ / solute-feeding CZ

**Excluded from melt growth entirely:** BN, AlN, GaN, InN (wurtzite nitrides — no congruent melt at practical pressures; grown by vapor-phase, ammonothermal, or HPHT solution methods), and AlP, BP, BAs, BSb (decompose at or before their melting points under normal conditions).
