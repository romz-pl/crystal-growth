# Electromagnetic Czochralski (EMCZ) Method

## Overview

The Electromagnetic Czochralski (EMCZ) method is a variant of the standard Czochralski (CZ) crystal-pulling process, developed to control melt convection and rotation **without mechanically rotating the crucible**. A new method for growing silicon crystals by using electromagnetic force to rotate the melt without crucible rotation was developed, and it was called electromagnetic Czochralski (EMCZ) growth. It was pioneered by Watanabe and co-workers in the late 1990s specifically for silicon single-crystal growth, and has since become an important subject of both experimental and numerical study in the semiconductor crystal-growth community.

## Working Principle

The core physical mechanism is the generation of a Lorentz force that drives melt rotation electromagnetically rather than mechanically:

- An electromagnetic force in the azimuthal direction is generated in the melt by the interaction between an electric current (I) passed through the melt in the radial direction and a vertical magnetic field (B).
- This radial current × vertical field interaction (**I × B**) produces an azimuthal (rotational) Lorentz force that stirs the melt in a controlled, contactless way, mimicking or replacing the effect of crucible rotation.
- The melt rotation rate (ωm) can be continuously varied from 0 to over 105 rpm as I is varied from 0 to 8 A and B from 0 to 0.1 T, giving fine, decoupled control over stirring intensity independent of any mechanical motion.
- A vertical (axial) magnetic field is introduced into the melt together with an electrical system generating a horizontal (radial) current passing through the melt, and the melt rotation rate can be effectively controlled purely by varying the current and/or field strength.

This is conceptually different from other magnetic-field-assisted CZ variants:

- **MCZ (Magnetic-field-applied CZ)**: uses a static magnetic field (vertical, transverse, or cusp-shaped) primarily to *damp* thermal/buoyant convection via the Lorentz braking force on the naturally convecting melt.
- **EMS-CZ / rotating-field methods**: use a rotating electromagnetic field to actively drive controlled convection in the melt, pulling crystals without mechanical rotation of either the crystal or crucible.
- **EMCZ**: uses a *combination* of a steady vertical field and an imposed radial current to generate azimuthal stirring — a distinct current-injection approach rather than a purely rotating-field or purely damping field.

## Historical Development

- The original demonstration grew 30-mm-diameter dislocation-free silicon single crystals under two parameter sets: I = 2.0 A, B = 0.05 T (giving ωm ≈ 105 rpm), and I = 0.2 A, B = 0.1 T (giving ωm ≈ 15 rpm).
- Measured oxygen concentrations were 8×10¹⁷ atoms/cm³ at the high rotation rate and 1×10¹⁷ atoms/cm³ at the low rotation rate, with radial oxygen distributions in both cases more homogeneous than in crystals grown by conventional CZ.
- The technique was subsequently scaled up: researchers demonstrated that EMCZ could precisely control oxygen concentration and distribution in commercial-size, 200-mm-diameter silicon crystals, showing the method's viability beyond small laboratory ingots. The authors concluded that EMCZ growth using a controlled magnetic field is an elegant way of preparing silicon single crystals with a homogeneous oxygen distribution, and noted it may be especially useful for large-diameter growth where both radial and axial oxygen inhomogeneity are otherwise problematic.
- Related patent literature from the same period describes analogous concepts: a method of loading a seed crystal and charge into a vessel, melting the charge, electromagnetically stirring the melt to promote uniformity, and slowly cooling the melt over the crystal to grow it, explicitly building on the EMCZ work of Watanabe et al.

## Motivation: Why Replace Crucible Rotation?

Conventional CZ growth relies on mechanical rotation of the crucible (and crystal) to homogenize the melt, control the boundary layer at the growth interface, and manage dopant/oxygen transport. This becomes problematic as crystal diameters increase:

- For a long period, the conventional technique used to grow CZ-Si crystal was to rotate the silicon melt in the crucible by rotating the crucible itself, but it became difficult to control the oxygen concentration and its distribution in the crystal as the diameter grown became larger.
- In the EMF (EMCZ) method, by contrast, the silicon melt in a stable, non-rotating crucible is rotated by the electromagnetic force generated by the interaction between horizontal electric current and vertical magnetic field.
- Because the melt rotation rate can be effectively controlled by varying the electromagnetic force strength — i.e., by adjusting the current and/or magnet strength — oxygen concentration and its distribution in the crystal become correspondingly tunable.

This decoupling of "stirring intensity" from "mechanical rotation rate" is the central practical advantage of EMCZ: it gives an extra, independent control knob for melt transport phenomena.

## Effects on Crystal Quality

### Oxygen control
Oxygen incorporation in CZ silicon comes primarily from dissolution of the silica (SiO₂) crucible wall, and its concentration/distribution is strongly governed by melt convection patterns. EMCZ's electromagnetically driven azimuthal flow gives direct, tunable leverage over this transport:

- Using an electric current of I = 0.4 A through the silicon melt in an axisymmetric vertical magnetic field of B = 0.05 T, an oxygen concentration of nearly 1×10¹⁸ atoms/cm³ with a homogeneous distribution was obtained in the grown crystals.
- Interactions with crucible rotation and additional fields matter: without a cusp magnetic field, oxygen content is smaller at low crucible rotation speed than at high rotation speed for both counter- and iso-rotation conditions, but this trend reverses when a cusp magnetic field is present. Since melt flow is retarded by the Lorentz force at high magnetic Reynolds number, diffusion plays a greater role in oxygen transport in the bulk melt, promoting oxygen transport from the crucible wall toward the melt surface and growth interface.

### Crystal–melt interface shape and defects
- When melt rotation induced by the electromagnetic force is in the same direction as the crystal rotation, the melt rotation makes the crystal–melt interface concave, and the critical growth rate for defect-free crystal increases; the melt convection induced by EMF also decreases the oxygen concentration. When the EMF-driven melt rotation is opposite to the crystal rotation, the effect on interface shape and oxygen behavior differs.
- Point-defect and dislocation studies found broadly similar behavior to conventionally-fielded CZ crystals: defects in EMCZ-Si crystals were studied for the first time using multi-chroic infrared light-scattering tomography, and spot-like dislocation clusters were observed, similar to those seen in CZ-Si crystals grown under three different cusp magnetic field configurations. Because the defect character and the V/G ratio (growth rate over axial temperature gradient) used were similar to those cases, the defects in EMCZ-Si crystal were likewise concluded to be of interstitial type, agglomerating into dislocation lines and/or clusters.
- Interface behavior is sensitive to the specific field topology chosen: with a cusp magnetic field, hot melt is easily transported to the solid–liquid interface, causing strong interface deflection, whereas with a vertical magnetic field configuration (as in classic EMCZ), radial heat transport is instead suppressed by the melt rotation.

## Numerical Modeling Considerations

Because EMCZ inherently breaks axisymmetric assumptions (a radial current path is not azimuthally symmetric in the way a simple vertical-field CZ system is), it poses distinctive challenges for simulation:

- The electromagnetic Czochralski method is a typical example where a fully three-dimensional model is necessary for a detailed study of heat transfer in the melt–crystal subsystem, and reduction to a two-dimensional model is not possible.
- Earlier simulation studies of fluid flow in EMCZ configurations were limited to relatively small crucible diameters (≈7.5 cm), corresponding to Grashof numbers of 10⁷–10⁸, and only considered a vertical magnetic field configuration, whereas industrial-scale Czochralski melts have Grashof numbers exceeding 10¹⁰ — meaning full-scale, industrially relevant EMCZ simulation requires substantial computational resources, motivating the use of parallel, large-scale 3D thermal-fluid-electromagnetic coupled solvers (finite element/finite volume MHD models coupling Navier–Stokes, heat transfer, and Maxwell's equations for the induced Lorentz force field).
- More broadly, direct experimental visualization has complemented simulation: X-ray radiography combined with large-scale numerical computation has been used to directly observe and simulate molten silicon flow during crystal growth under magnetic fields, reviewing the effects of vertical and cusp-shaped magnetic fields on melt flow during CZ growth and comparing experimental and simulated results.

## Comparison with Other Magnetic CZ Variants

| Method | Field configuration | Primary mechanism | Typical goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conventional CZ** | None | Mechanical crucible/crystal counter-rotation | Baseline melt homogenization |
| **VMF-CZ** (vertical magnetic field, static) | Uniform axial DC field | Lorentz damping of buoyant convection | Suppress turbulence, but can hinder oxygen release from the surface |
| **CMF-CZ / MCZ** (cusp magnetic field) | Two opposed coils, cusp near melt surface | Selective damping near center, less near walls | Balance convection suppression with oxygen control |
| **Transverse field CZ** | Horizontal DC field | Suppresses convection and surface undulation | Flatten growth interface, reduce oxygen |
| **EMS-CZ (rotating field)** | Rotating electromagnetic field | Actively drives melt rotation, replacing all mechanical rotation | Pull crystals with zero mechanical rotation |
| **EMCZ** | Static vertical field **+ imposed radial DC current** | Lorentz force (I × B) drives azimuthal melt rotation | Decouple/control melt rotation rate independent of crucible rotation; homogenize oxygen at large diameter |

## Practical Significance

- The method was proposed as one that could be easily adopted for growing large-diameter silicon crystals, and this promise was validated at the 200-mm wafer scale, relevant to real semiconductor manufacturing needs. The motivation was explicitly tied to ULSI (ultra-large-scale-integration) device processing, which requires large-diameter, high-quality silicon wafers with homogeneously distributed oxygen at concentrations suited to device fabrication.
- The technique remains primarily documented in the silicon photovoltaic/semiconductor crystal-growth literature (Watanabe et al., Hoshi, Takada, and related Japanese groups), with follow-on simulation studies (e.g., Vizman and co-workers) extending understanding of EMCZ melt dynamics for 200-mm configurations.

## Summary

EMCZ is best understood as a **contactless melt-stirring technique** for Czochralski growth: rather than rotating the crucible, an engineer applies a static vertical magnetic field across a melt through which a controlled radial (or horizontal) electric current is passed, generating an azimuthal Lorentz force that spins the melt itself. This gives an independent handle on melt convection and rotation rate, which historically has been the main lever for controlling oxygen incorporation and homogeneity, crystal–melt interface shape, and defect formation in CZ silicon — while avoiding the mechanical complexity and constraints of physically rotating large, heavy crucibles at industrial scale.
